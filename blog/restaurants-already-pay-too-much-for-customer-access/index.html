Restaurants Already Pay Too Much for Customer Access

Marketplaces created enormous value for restaurants, but they also normalized paying heavily just to reach customers. Agent-driven ordering could eventually create new options.

Marketplaces changed the industry for a reason

Online marketplaces changed the restaurant industry forever.

Before delivery platforms became mainstream, many restaurants struggled with discovery, logistics, and digital ordering. Marketplaces helped solve those problems at scale. They brought customers, streamlined fulfillment, and made online ordering incredibly convenient.

That value was real. And for many restaurants, those platforms became essential growth channels.

Another tradeoff became normal too

Over time, something else became normalized as well: paying significant percentages simply to access customers.

For years, restaurants have accepted the tradeoff:

In return
  • More visibility
  • More convenience
  • More order volume
The tradeoff
  • Marketplace fees
  • Reduced customer ownership
  • Limited direct relationships
  • Dependence on third-party ecosystems

Again, this was not necessarily a bad thing. The infrastructure marketplaces built created enormous value for consumers and restaurants alike.

Technology is creating new possibilities

But technology continues to evolve. And the rise of autonomous ordering will eventually create new options.

If AI assistants increasingly begin placing orders on behalf of consumers, the structure of discovery itself starts to change. Instead of customers manually browsing inside centralized marketplace apps, intelligent systems will coordinate purchases through connected restaurant infrastructure directly.

That shift matters because marketplaces were built around controlling discovery. Restaurants paid significant percentages because the platforms owned the customer interface: the browsing experience, the rankings, and the visibility layer itself.

Humans

Browse emotionally through photos, rankings, placements, cravings, and brand signals.

Agents

Optimize operationally around fulfillment reliability, menu structure, pricing, consistency, and system compatibility.


An AI system does not care about flashy UI, endless scrolling menus, or viral food photography. It cares about fulfillment reliability, structured menu data, operational consistency, pricing, and system compatibility.

That changes where discovery happens.

A different kind of ecosystem could emerge

That opens the door to a different kind of ecosystem. One where restaurants become accessible across many different agents, assistants, operating systems, and ordering platforms simultaneously, not just through a single marketplace interface.

And because autonomous systems evaluate businesses differently than humans do, different qualities start to matter:

Structured menu data
Direct POS connectivity
Clean integrations
Predictable fulfillment
Operational consistency

As autonomous discovery grows, infrastructure itself starts becoming part of discoverability.

This does not mean marketplaces disappear

Importantly, this does not mean marketplaces disappear.

Discovery still matters. Convenience still matters. Delivery networks still matter. Humans will still browse, explore, and order.

But marketplaces may no longer remain the exclusive gatekeepers of digital demand.

What restaurants could regain

The future could look more like an open infrastructure layer where many different agents, assistants, and ordering systems interact directly with restaurants.

And if that happens, restaurants may no longer be dependent on a single centralized discovery platform to reach digital customers.

Control over discovery becomes more distributed.

Why this moment matters

The internet moved commerce online. Marketplaces centralized digital discovery. Agent-driven commerce changes where discovery happens.

And for the first time in years, restaurants may no longer need to depend on a single marketplace interface as the primary gateway to digital customers.
